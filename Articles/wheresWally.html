Where's Wally? - Just Google him

Part 1 - Privacy and its impacts in this digital age.

By Josh Ryland

After a long day at work, Wally would get home, put the kettle on, and close the curtains. Sitting down to enjoy a hot tea in the privacy of his own home, safe and secure knowing he was finally alone. Privacy is no longer this straightforward. The digital age has altered the very definition of privacy and its impacts on individuals, families, and businesses. If Wally were to close his curtains, his phone would still broadcast his location to the world, and companies would record his conversations behind closed doors. His casual browsing of the internet would be analyzed, saved, and sold to the highest bidder. Even the kettle which he boiled his tea would be connected to the internet, telling whoever was listening to Wally's actions. This is not some dystopian future, playing along the lines of some Truman Show-Esque parody. Modern-day smartphones, AI, high-speed internet, and the Internet of Technology (IoT) have made it possible for every action to be recorded and every movement to be tracked. All under the pretense of convenience, marketing, and product improvement. Moreover, humanity knows it; they have fallen for the Privacy Paradox (Barth & De Jong, 2017; Gerber et al, 2018).

This literature piece intends to analyze privacy in the digital age.

Social Media, IoT, technological advances, and policymaking will all be examined, revealing just how privacy is impacted and how this pertains to the Privacy Paradox. A literature review will first examine what the Privacy Paradox is and other relevant academic journals in regard to privacy. Such as Sicari et al. 's (2015) article on the IoT and its impacts on individual privacy. Following this will be several sections analyzing theories around the paradox. Finally, a discussion will examine the future of privacy and how privacy paradox is leading to a 'Fear is the Mind Killer' mentality and extends beyond behavior online.

Literature Review
The Privacy Paradox is as Gerber et al. (2016) 's states, has been a topic of discussion for at least ten years (Smith et al., 2011; Zafeiropouloi et al., 2013; Acquisti et al., 2007; Cho et al., 2010). They say that the paradox describes the dichotomy of privacy attitude around inform and privacy behavior. On the one hand, a user will express concern around personnel data and will state an urge to protect their data. While simultaneously posting details of their every day online for all to see, giving up personnel data freely and making minimal effort to protect themselves through cookies deletion, communication encryption, etc. (Gerber et al., 2016). Gerber et al. 's (2016) paper to examine all the relevant research to establish a more precise understanding of the paradox. They find that attitude towards privacy, concerns, perceived risk, and behavioral intentions are the main factors hinting towards social factors determining a user participating in the paradox. Gerber et al. (2018) conclude that despite these findings, that no definitive conclusion can be drawn. Current research examines certain facets or areas of privacy in the digital domain. Currently, there is no clear link apart from social constructs, for this paradox spans boundaries.

Barth & De Jong (2017) also attempt a systematic review of the literature regarding the privacy paradox. They state that the electronic transference of data in the data age is at an unprecedented rate, allowing for unwitting users to be observed around the clock due to constant network connectivity. It is leading to extensive scale collection of data and the term 'Big Data' being coined (Barth & De Jong, 2017). Despite this and increasing fears due to hacking data scandals and misuse, users are more conscious of data privacy but not acting upon it. This leads to the privacy paradox. The results of their review conclude that rational-decisionmaking is not the sole reason for users to undergo privacy practices. Sometimes a clean user dashboard presented on an app will be enough for a user to divulge all information as this professional and clean outlook fosters trust in the user's mind. Therefore, it is proposed that this area be investigated as privacy is fast becoming a global crisis.

The final pieces in this literature review are that of Sicari et al. (2015) and Weinberg et al. (2015), who, like Caron et al. (2016), turn their academic lens to IoT, and the balance of convenience vs. privacy. Sicari et al. (2015) analyze the road ahead for IoT and privacy, stating that privacy customization will have to become a mandatory requirement if IoT is to spread. They state that this is hard, though, due to the heterogeneous nature and multitude of IoT devices (Sicari et al., 2015). This provides a clear link to the privacy paradox and relations with privacy. It shows that IoT may be too complicated or varied which, coupled with a lack of customization, causes users to feel alienated. Resulting in them not bothering with their privacy as it is not convenient. Weinberg et al. (2015) also take up this claim, stating IoT is allowing for unprecedented contact between consumers, firms, and machines. IoT, the state, 'does not exist' without data analysis and generation, which leads to substantial privacy concerns (Weinberg et al., 2015, p 620). Finally, they state that privacy by design is a possible solution to this issue. They are highlighting a possible solution for the privacy paradox, alluding that it may occur due to a lack of good design. Laziness and unintuitive design are causing consumers engaging in the privacy paradox.

So a multitude of academic journals are both directly referring to the privacy paradox and also referring to it indirectly. Stating how new IoT devices in the digital age are causing privacy concerns. But where does that lead us?
